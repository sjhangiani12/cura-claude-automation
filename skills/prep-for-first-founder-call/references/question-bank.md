# Question patterns for first founder calls

These are PATTERNS, not actual questions. Each pattern, when filled in with specifics from the founder's deck/email, becomes a sharp question. Avoid asking these in their template form — always ground them in real data from the founder.

## The five question buckets

A good first-call brief has roughly one question from each of these buckets:

1. **Probe the strongest claim** — falsify the best thing they said
2. **Probe the weakest signal** — kindly surface what doesn't add up
3. **Test thesis fit** — does their world map to the GP's?
4. **Open them up** — let them show how they think
5. **The "what would change your mind" question** — ask what kills the deal

## Bucket 1: Probe the strongest claim

Pattern: take the most impressive metric or claim, ask the question whose answer would tell you if it's real.

- "You said [metric] is [value]. What's the breakdown — is that [dimension A] or [dimension B] driving it?"
- "You mentioned [customer A, customer B, customer C]. Which of those was the hardest to land, and why?"
- "Your deck says [strong claim]. What would prove that wrong?"
- "[Number] feels notable for this stage. How are you thinking about retention behind that growth?"

What this signals to the founder: you're paying attention, you can read a deck, and you don't take metrics on faith.

## Bucket 2: Probe the weakest signal

Pattern: name something specific that doesn't quite add up. Ask kindly. Founder reaction tells you a lot.

- "I noticed [gap in their resume / weird pattern in funding history / missing co-founder context]. Walk me through that."
- "Your last role at [Co] was [N months]. What happened there?"
- "[Founder]'s LinkedIn shows [X] but the deck implies [Y]. Help me square those."
- "You mentioned competitor [Y] but not competitor [Z]. How do you think about [Z]?"

Critical: ASK KINDLY. The point is to see how they handle the weak spot, not to corner them. A defensive founder is a flag; a self-aware founder is gold.

## Bucket 3: Test thesis fit

Pattern: ask the question whose answer determines whether the GP's thesis applies.

If the GP's thesis is about, say, **"infrastructure for AI-native applications,"** the question might be:
- "How much of your value is in the infra layer vs. the application logic on top?"
- "If everyone could spin up AI-native apps trivially, would your wedge survive?"

If the GP's thesis is about **"founders who deeply understand a specific industry,"**:
- "How many years did you spend in [industry] before starting this?"
- "What's the thing about [industry] that everyone gets wrong?"

If the thesis is about **"contrarian markets,"**:
- "Why are most VCs not investing in this category?"
- "What do you know that the consensus doesn't?"

Adapt the pattern to the actual config thesis.

## Bucket 4: Open them up

Pattern: give the founder room to show how they think. Open-ended, no right answer.

- "What would have to be true for this not to work?"
- "What's the most surprising thing you've learned in the last 6 months building this?"
- "If I gave you $5M tomorrow with no expectations, what's the first thing you'd do with it?"
- "Walk me through the decision to start this — what was the moment?"
- "What's a hard problem you haven't figured out yet?"

What you're learning: do they think rigorously? Are they intellectually honest? Do they self-criticize?

## Bucket 5: What would change your mind

Pattern: ask what would kill the deal — for them.

- "If your traction stalled at [current level] for 6 months, what would you do?"
- "What's the customer feedback that would make you pivot?"
- "Under what conditions would you decide to shut this down?"

Founders who can answer this thoughtfully are dramatically better bets than founders who can't conceive of failure.

## Anti-patterns (don't ask these)

- **"What's your moat?"** — Generic, over-asked. Better: "What would happen if [specific competitor] decided to build this exactly?"
- **"Why now?"** — Generic. Only ask if you have a specific timing thesis to test.
- **"What's your TAM?"** — Founder will quote the deck. Better: "What's the SAM you can realistically capture in 5 years?"
- **"Tell me about yourself"** — Waste of 5 minutes. The GP has the LinkedIn.
- **"What's your fundraising plan?"** — Save for end. Or skip — the deck says it.
- **"How much are you raising?"** — Save for end. Or skip — the deck says it.
- **"What's your team?"** — Read the deck.
- **"How do you make money?"** — Read the deck.

## Sector-specific patterns

### Dev tools / infrastructure
- "Where does your product fit in the buyer's existing toolchain?"
- "What's the unit of sale — seat, usage, contract?"
- "How long is the median sales cycle today?"

### Vertical SaaS
- "What's the first piece of software your customer would rip out if they had to cut spend?"
- "Are you replacing a system, augmenting one, or creating a new category?"

### Consumer
- "What's your D1, D7, D30 retention?"
- "What % of users are paying for the product currently? Where do you want that to be in 12 months?"

### AI / ML
- "What's your model strategy — fine-tune, RAG, agentic, or just well-prompted commodity models?"
- "What gets harder if foundation model capability doubles in the next 12 months?"
- "How are you thinking about defensibility when the underlying models are commodity?"

### Enterprise software
- "What's the procurement process you've encountered with your closest customer?"
- "Who's the actual user vs. the buyer vs. the budget holder?"

### Fintech
- "What's the regulatory posture? Are you a regulated entity, partner-banked, or neither?"
- "What's your loss rate / fraud rate today, and how does that scale?"

### Marketplace
- "What's the classic chicken-and-egg problem in your category, and how are you solving it?"
- "What's your take rate, and is it sustainable at scale?"

## Stage-specific patterns

### Pre-seed (no traction yet)
- "How will you know in 90 days if this is working?"
- "What's the riskiest assumption that would fail fastest?"

### Seed (some traction)
- "What changed in the last 60 days that's working better than expected?"
- "What metric are you most worried about?"

### Series A (proven model)
- "What does scale look like — what's the bottleneck at 5x your current run rate?"
- "How are you thinking about the team you'll need 12 months from now?"
